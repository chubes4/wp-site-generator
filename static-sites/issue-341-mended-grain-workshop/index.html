Start with the problem

Choose the mark before you choose the tool.

Most repair mistakes come from buying by product name. These routes begin with what you see on the furniture, then narrow by wood species and finish type.

Ring marks

White heat haze or water blooms

For dining tables, sideboards, and coffee tables with cloudy finish trapped above the grain.

 Loose joints

Chairs that wobble but still fit together

Clean glue lines, reset rungs, and clamp without overbuilding the repair.

 Scratched grain

Walnut and oak color loss

Blend surface scratches with species-matched pigments, wax fill, and finishing cloths.

 Veneer chips

Missing corners and lifted edges

Patch small losses, soften edges, and keep the repair honest rather than invisible.

Material notes

Close-up supplies, labeled like a careful bench drawer.

Repair material is easier to trust when you can see what it does. These cards keep species, finish, and handling details visible before purchase.

Raw oak · open grain

Oak fill behaves in ridges

Use warmer wax on worn edges and avoid flattening grain texture unless the scratch catches a fingernail.

Walnut · dark pore

Color depth comes in passes

Layer pigment cloth first, then wax. A single dark stick often turns a scratch into a stripe.

Shellac · amber repair

Soften cloudy finish slowly

Heat haze often sits above the wood. Test a hidden spot before reaching for sandpaper.

Hide glue · reversible

Old joints deserve compatible glue

Warm, clean, and clamp square. Reversible repairs protect the next person who works on the chair.

The workshop method

A calmer path from damaged to useful.

Every kit is built to reduce intimidation without pretending repair is magic. The cards slow the work down and keep the next step clear.

  1. 01

    Identify the finish

    Use included test dots and plain-language cues before applying anything to the visible surface.

  2. 02

    Work the smallest area

    Repair only the mark, joint, or edge that needs attention. Preserve patina wherever it is still sound.

  3. 03

    Blend, cure, then judge

    Give wax, shellac, and glue their correct time before adding more. Instructions use tabular quantities and cure windows.

  4. 04

    Keep a repair record

    Each card leaves space for species, finish, product batch, and what worked for the next maintenance pass.
