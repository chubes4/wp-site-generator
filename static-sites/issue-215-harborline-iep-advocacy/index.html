Services

Practical advocacy before, during, and after school meetings.

Harborline works collaboratively when possible and firmly when needed, keeping the focus on the child’s documented needs and the family’s next decision.

IEP meeting preparation

Records review, issue spotting, parent agenda drafting, goal and service questions, and meeting-day talking points.

Document review

Plain-language review of evaluations, progress reports, prior written notices, draft plans, eligibility notes, and meeting summaries.

Evaluation support

Help understanding school evaluation proposals, timelines, data gaps, and when families may want to discuss an independent evaluation.

504 plan guidance

Accommodation review for ADHD, anxiety-related school avoidance, medical needs, learning differences, and access concerns.

Progress review, updated concerns, revised goals, service questions, and preparation for changes in placement or support.

Written summary review, action-step tracking, communication drafts, and next-step planning after the meeting ends.

A steadier process

From intake to next steps, every stage has a job.

  1. 01

    Intake and fit check

    Share the meeting date, school concern, child’s grade range, and what decision is in front of you.

  2. 02

    Record review

    Upload or gather the current plan, evaluations, progress reports, school notices, and recent email threads.

  3. 03

    Goal-setting

    Define what you need to understand, what you want the team to address, and what should be documented.

  4. 04

    Meeting preparation

    Build a parent agenda, questions, suggested language, and a calm sequence for raising concerns.

  5. 05

    Attendance options

    Choose preparation-only support, phone/video consultation, or meeting participation when appropriate.

  6. 06

    Written next steps

    Leave with a summary of decisions, unresolved items, follow-up dates, and records to request or preserve.

Credible, careful advocacy

Collaborative with school teams. Firm about the record.

Harborline is built for families who need an organized second set of eyes before signing a plan or walking into a high-stakes meeting. The practice centers preparation, documentation, and respectful communication.

“The goal is not to make the room louder. It is to make the child’s needs clearer, the questions sharper, and the next steps harder to overlook.”

What proof looks like here

  • Training in special-education procedures and family meeting preparation
  • Experience reviewing school records, draft plans, and evaluation materials
  • Professional memberships, references, and background details shared during consultation
  • Parent feedback focused on clarity, preparation, and calmer communication

Case-style example

When progress reports and services do not match

A family may bring a plan that lists services, but progress notes still show stalled reading, escalating avoidance, or unclear goals. Harborline helps compare the documents, identify questions, and prepare a request for clearer data and next steps.

Parent feedback theme

“We knew what to ask next.”

Families often describe the relief of having their records organized into meeting priorities, follow-up items, and language they can use with the school team.

Local familiarity

Built for coastal county school conversations.

Harborline supports families across the county and nearby districts, with careful attention to district calendars, meeting rhythms, transportation realities, and the documentation families are typically asked to manage. Local familiarity helps preparation feel concrete, but no advocate can promise a specific school outcome.

What to bring to a meeting

Current plan, recent evaluations, progress reports, work samples, relevant emails, outside provider notes, and your top three concerns.

Warning signs a plan may need review

Vague goals, missing service details, unclear progress data, repeated removals from class, or supports that exist only informally.

Independent evaluation questions

When school data feels incomplete, families may need to ask what was assessed, what was not, and what additional expertise could clarify.
